# Portbahn Islay & Bothán Jura Retreat - Tone of Voice Skill
**Version:** 1.3
**Date:** 2026-03-02
**Purpose:** Editorial voice guide for all content creation across PBI and BJR properties

---

## Core Voice Principles

### **Personal, Simple, Clear, Evocative**

**Not:** Corporate accommodation copy
**But:** How Pi and Lynton would naturally describe their homes and the islands

**Not:** "The property features..."
**But:** "Our..."

**Not:** "Guests will find..."
**But:** "You'll discover..." or "We've included..."

**The ultimate test:** Would you say this to someone standing in the kitchen? If it sounds like a press release, it's wrong. If it sounds like a content writer who's read the brief, it's still wrong. It needs to sound like Pi and Lynton talking.

## The Voice DNA

### **1. PERSONAL & DIRECT**

**We are:**
- Pi and Lynton managing three family homes (not purpose-built AirBnBs)
- Hosts who care deeply because these are real homes with real stories
- Present and involved (not absent landlords)

**The homes:**
- **Portbahn House:** Our own family home (Pi and Lynton's). Always use "Portbahn House" to distinguish from Portbahn Beach and the Portbahn Islay brand.
- **Shorefield:** The Jackson family's home—they built it, planted the trees, created the wetlands and bird hides, filled it with their paintings, books, and curios from travels. Use "Shorefield" in body copy, "Shorefield Eco House" for page titles only.
- **Curlew Cottage:** Alan's family retreat for years, now opened to guests—filled with his family's personal effects and "holiday things". Always use "Curlew Cottage", never just "Curlew".

**Critical distinction:** NOT purpose-created modern/neutral/soulless AirBnB businesses. These are family homes with personal touches, run by us with that in mind.

---

### **"I" vs "We" — When to Use Each**

This is nuanced and deliberate. The default voice is **"we"** (Pi and Lynton together), with **"I"** reserved for specific contexts.

**Use "we" for:**
- General property descriptions ("Our properties sit around Bruichladdich village")
- Recommendations ("We recommend booking 12 weeks ahead")
- Shared knowledge ("We know the island well")
- Hospitality ("We've been welcoming guests since 2017")
- Most content by default

**Use "I" for:**
- Crisis/support situations where Pi is the direct contact ("Contact me immediately")
- Personal anecdotes or opinions ("I'd urge you to visit Bruichladdich first")
- Direct reassurance ("I've helped dozens of guests navigate this")
- When signing off personally ("Pi, Lynton and Amba")

**Use "I'm Pi..." sparingly:**
- About page opening (appropriate for introduction)
- NOT as a standard opener for guide pages (too in-your-face)

**Example of the balance:**
> "Having lived and worked on Islay for a number of years, we know the island well and want to share a few of our favourite things to do here... If your ferry is cancelled, contact me immediately—I've helped dozens of guests navigate CalMac disruptions."

The "we" establishes shared knowledge; the "I" makes crisis support feel personal and direct.

**Examples from live content:**

❌ Switching randomly between I/we in the same paragraph without reason

### **2. LEAD WITH EMOTION, NOT GEOGRAPHY**

**The opening sentence of any hub or guide page should make you want to read on — not inform you.**

Facts and entities earn their place once the reader is engaged. Geography, history, and logistics are the body — not the hook.

❌ **Wrong:** "Islay lies in the southern Hebrides, an island of 3,000 people with ten working whisky distilleries."
✅ **Right:** "We love Islay and we love sharing its best bits with people."

❌ **Wrong:** "The ferry from Kennacraig takes two hours to reach Islay."
✅ **Right:** "The ferry is actually one of the most enjoyable parts of the holiday."

The emotion comes first. The facts follow naturally once you've got the reader's attention.

### **3. SCOTTISH UNDERSTATEMENT**

**Understatement beats emphasis. Always.**

This is not just a stylistic preference — it's part of the authentic voice. Scottish understatement signals a local, signals confidence, and is far more trustworthy than superlatives.

✅ "A pretty good ratio" (for ten distilleries on one island of 3,000 people)
✅ "Yes, we do have the odd rainy day!"
✅ "Not a bad way to arrive"
✅ "We do the crossing all the time and it never loses its magic" (not "it's breathtaking every time")

❌ "Remarkable by any measure"
❌ "A truly extraordinary concentration"
❌ "Breathtaking views you'll never forget"

When you feel the urge to use a superlative, try understatement first. Nine times out of ten it's stronger.

---

### **4. CASUAL CONFIDENCE MARKERS**

**These are the phrases that signal a person is talking, not a writer composing.**

They should appear naturally in guide and hub content — not forced, but not edited out either. They include:

- "to name a few"
- "and yes, of course"
- "we really do urge you"
- "it really is excellent"
- "I know - you don't expect good food on a ferry, but it really is"
- "please do use us"
- "and yes, we do have the odd one!"

The test: read the sentence aloud. If it sounds like something you'd say to a guest in the kitchen, it's right. If it sounds like something a marketing team approved, it's wrong.

---

### **5. NATURAL SPEECH PATTERNS — DO NOT SMOOTH**

**This is a specific failure mode: identifying a good voice feature, then editing it out.**

Intentional repetition, double modifiers, and conversational rhythm are features of authentic host voice, not errors to correct.

✅ "Islay is a lot, lot easier with your own car." (double modifier — keep it)
✅ "Keep an eye on the weather, always keep an eye on the weather!" (repetition for emphasis — keep it)
✅ "Two hours on open water, Islay appearing on the horizon." (fragment — keep it)

These patterns occur naturally when someone is speaking from experience. Smoothing them out creates content-writer copy. Leave them in.

**The rule:** If it sounds like a grammar teacher would correct it, ask whether the "correction" removes the humanity. If yes, leave it as written.

---

### **6. PERSONAL BIOGRAPHICAL DETAIL**

**Named personal details are the highest trust signal. No content writer can fabricate them.**

This is the most powerful voice tool in the set — and the hardest to fake. It signals authentic host knowledge instantly.

✅ Named animals: "crazy working cockers — first Obi, now Pixel"
✅ Children's ages and history: "Our children were 2½ and 4 weeks old when we moved to Islay. They're now 12 and 14."
✅ Direct quotes from family: "When we ask our children whether they'd like to move to the mainland they always say 'No way!'"
✅ Specific dates and events: "We came here in 2014 and Portbahn was our home for several years"

When writing any page where personal connection is relevant, ask: is there a named person, animal, age, date, or direct quote we can use? If yes, use it.

These details are held by Lynton and Pi — always escalate to get them, never invent them.

---

### **7. NAMED GEOGRAPHIC SPECIFICITY**

**Specific named places beat atmospheric description every time — for voice AND for AEO.**

A named landmark does three things: it signals you were there, it creates a vivid image, and it gives AI retrieval systems a specific entity to anchor.

❌ "Watching the island appear on the horizon"
✅ "The island getting ever bigger on the horizon, the Paps of Jura dominating the skyline"

❌ "Beautiful coastal scenery on the drive"
✅ "A coffee at Loch Fyne or Inveraray, let the kids have a play in the playground at Lochgilphead"

❌ "Atmospheric winter views"
✅ "The Paps of Jura from the sofa, across the loch to Bowmore"

When writing evocative content, always ask: what is the specific name of the thing I'm describing? Use it.

---

### **7. FRAME CONSTRAINTS AS GIFTS**

**The absence of something can be the feature.**

Islay is remote, slow, and low on infrastructure. That's not a weakness to apologise for — it's why people come.

✅ "Two hours on open water with nothing else to do" (the constraint — no wifi, no errands — is the point)
✅ "Safe beaches where you can wind the clock back fifty years"
✅ "Quiet roads, empty beaches" (absence of crowds = feature)
✅ "It's hard to get here, but that's the point"

When you encounter something that seems like a limitation (ferry-only access, limited shops, no phone signal), look for the gift in it before writing around it.

---

### **8. "ACTUALLY" AS EXPECTATION INVERSION**

**Use "actually" to subvert expectations — it signals honest surprise and earned conviction.**

"The ferry is actually one of the most enjoyable parts of the holiday" works because it acknowledges that a two-hour ferry might seem like a chore, then inverts the expectation. The "actually" tells the reader: I know what you're thinking, and I'm telling you from experience you're wrong.

✅ "I know — you don't expect good food on a ferry, but it really is excellent"
✅ "The ferry is actually one of the most enjoyable parts of the holiday"
✅ "CalMac does the food so well — genuinely, not faint praise"

Use sparingly — one per section maximum. Overuse kills the effect.

---

### **9. AUDIENCE-AWARE WRITING**

**The hub page is a helicopter view for different audiences. Name who each option is for.**

Islay visitors are not one audience. They are families, whisky groups, birdwatchers, cyclists, solo travellers, couples. Each has different travel needs, different optimal seasons, different priorities. Hub pages should help each audience self-select without making them read everything.

**Pattern:**
- Name the audience ("for families especially", "for whisky groups", "for solo travellers and couples")
- State the honest implication ("you need your car — Islay is a lot, lot easier with one")
- Be direct about when an option doesn't work ("the flight is £500+ each way for a family of four — not realistic")

❌ Generic: "There are several ways to reach Islay."
✅ Audience-aware: "For most guests — families especially — the CalMac ferry from Kennacraig is how you'll get to Islay."

This is not about segmentation copy — it's about being honest and useful to the person actually reading.

---

### **10. QUIRKY / ECCENTRIC / ECLECTIC** (Without Being Weird)

**Islay (PBI):** Family homes with personality
**Jura (BJR):** A passion project, our DNA, built with hard work

**Voice characteristics:**
- Personality visible: "Shorefield: bird books, world travel mementos"
- Owner lens: "our favourite walks", not "Jura walks"
- Honest character: "a bit tired, a lot loved", "quirky charm"
- Real details: "sunken trampoline", "bird hides we created"

❌ Generic: "The accommodation includes books and games"
✅ Personal: "Grab the binoculars from the study, the board games are in the sitting room, and there's a fire to light on a rainy afternoon"

---

### **11. HONEST TRANSPARENCY**

**We don't hide imperfections — we frame them authentically**

✅ "Shorefield isn't for everyone... well-loved home, a little tired, filled with charm and character"
✅ "We point guests who prefer modern finishes to Portbahn"
✅ "The twin is the smallest room - fine for kids or a short stay, but couples might prefer the other bedrooms"

**Pattern:**
1. State the fact (imperfection)
2. Contextualize (why it's this way)
3. Help guest self-select (who it's for / not for)

---

### **12. WARM & REASSURING** (Especially in Crisis)

**Pi's legendary ferry support:**
✅ "We've got your back"
✅ "Contact me immediately — I've done this dozens of times"
✅ "We've never had a booking collapse due to CalMac"
✅ "We can often put you up in one of our other properties if you get stranded"

**Specific offers beat generic reassurance.** Not just "we'll help" — tell them what help actually looks like.

**Crisis voice pattern:**
1. Acknowledge the problem ("Ferry cancellations happen")
2. Show experience ("I've helped dozens of guests")
3. Give clear action ("Contact me immediately")
4. Reassure outcome with specific help ("We've never had a booking collapse — and we can often put you up in one of our other properties")

---

### **13. EVOCATIVE WITHOUT BEING FLOWERY**

**We paint pictures with specific named things, not adjectives**

✅ "The Paps of Jura dominating the skyline"
✅ "You're a 5-minute walk from Bruichladdich Distillery along the coastal cycle path"
✅ "On a clear day you can see the Paps of Jura from the sofa and look across the loch to Bowmore"

**But never:** Purple prose, over-adjectives, "once in a lifetime", "unforgettable"

**Pattern:**
- One evocative phrase per paragraph maximum
- Ground it in named, specific places (not "beautiful views" — name the view)
- Use guest language: "hidden gem", "home from home"

## Punctuation & Typography

### **Hyphens and Dashes**

**Use spaced hyphens ( - ) not em dashes (—)**

✅ "Shorefield is quirky - full of personality and charm"
✅ "Contact me immediately - I've helped dozens of guests"
✅ "It's not a styled rental - it's a real family home"

❌ "Shorefield is quirky—full of personality"

**Exception:** Hyphenated compound words have NO spaces:
✅ "well-loved", "family-friendly", "5-minute walk", "30-40 minutes"

## Sentence Structure

### **Mix of lengths, skewing shorter**

**Short for impact:**
✅ "It feels like the holiday has properly started."
✅ "We do the crossing all the time and it never loses its magic."
✅ "We've got your back."

**Fragments are acceptable for rhythm:**
✅ "Two hours on open water, Islay appearing on the horizon."
✅ "Quiet roads. Empty beaches. A thousand geese."

**Avoid:**
❌ Long, flowing sentences with multiple clauses that pack in too much information and lose clarity

## Content Type Variations

### **Hub Pages (islay-travel, explore-islay)**

**Purpose:** Helicopter view. Audience-aware signposting. Emotional orientation. NOT operational depth.

**Voice:** Warm, personal, honest about who each option is for. "We" throughout.

**What belongs on hub:**
- Why this topic matters
- Audience-aware guidance (who each option suits, who it doesn't)
- Honest implications (cost, practicality, limitations)
- Personal connection to the topic
- CTAs to each spoke page

**What does NOT belong on hub:**
- Specific timetables, prices, booking URLs
- Operational detail (route times, port specifics)
- Anything that replicates the spoke page

**Pattern:**
- Open with emotion or honest framing (not geography)
- Name the audiences and their optimal choice
- Personal conviction ("we do this all the time and it never loses its magic")
- Practical flag (without the detail — that's the spoke's job)
- CTA to spoke

**Gold standard — hub section:**
> For most guests - families especially - the CalMac ferry from Kennacraig is how you'll get to Islay. And if you want to explore, Islay is a lot, lot easier with your own car.

If anything changes, contact CalMac straight away and get in touch with us too - we've never had a booking collapse, and we can often put you up in one of our other properties if you get stranded. ## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### **❌ Smoothing natural speech patterns**
"Keep an eye on the weather" (cleaned up)
✅ **Keep:** "Keep an eye on the weather, always keep an eye on the weather!" (repetition is the point)

### **❌ Smoothing double modifiers**
"Islay is easier with a car" (cleaned up)
✅ **Keep:** "Islay is a lot, lot easier with your own car" (the double is the emphasis)

### **❌ Generic atmospheric description instead of named places**
"Watching the island appear on the horizon"
✅ **Fix:** "The island getting ever bigger on the horizon, the Paps of Jura dominating the skyline"

### **❌ Opening with geography instead of emotion**
"Islay lies in the southern Hebrides, 25 by 15 miles..."
✅ **Fix:** "We love Islay and we love sharing its best bits with people."

### **❌ Generic reassurance instead of specific help**
"We'll help you sort it out"
✅ **Fix:** "We've never had a booking collapse — and we can often put you up in one of our other properties if you get stranded"

### **❌ Too Generic**
"The properties offer a comfortable base for exploring the island"
✅ **Fix:** "Walk to Bruichladdich Distillery in 5 minutes, walk home for dinner, no one needs to stay sober"

### **❌ Too Corporate**
"Guests will find all amenities provided"
✅ **Fix:** "We've stocked the kitchen with everything you'll need"

### **❌ Over-Sold / Hyperbolic**
"An unforgettable once-in-a-lifetime experience"
✅ **Fix:** "The sunrise over Loch Indaal from the superking bedroom is something guests mention in nearly every review"

### **❌ Hiding Imperfections**
[No mention of worn carpets at Shorefield]
✅ **Fix:** "Shorefield is a bit tired in places — but guests who book here value character over modern finishes. It's a well-loved family home, not a styled rental."

### **❌ Audience-blind hub content**
"There are several ways to reach Islay."
✅ **Fix:** "For most guests — families especially — the CalMac ferry is how you'll get here. For solo travellers or couples traveling light, the flight is quick — but expensive, and there's no car at the other end."

### **❌ Worn idioms and content-writer reach phrases**

These sound like a writer filling space, not a host talking. Ban them:

| Phrase | Why it's wrong |
|--------|----------------|
| "it never disappoints" | Marketing cliché |
| "more times than we can count" | Overused idiom, feels written |
| "it pays to know" | Advice-column tone, impersonal |
| "written from real experience" | Defensive — a host just talks from experience, doesn't announce it |
| "once in a lifetime" | Hyperbolic |
| "unforgettable" | Hyperbolic |
| "a must-visit" | Listicle language |
| "nestled" | Estate agent cliché |
| "boasts" | PR speak |

When you reach for one of these, stop and ask: what is the actual specific thing I'm trying to say? Say that instead.

## Quality Checklist

Before publishing any content, verify:

**Voice:**
- [ ] Would you say this to someone standing in the kitchen? If it sounds like a press release, revise.
- [ ] Appropriate pronoun ("we" default, "I" for crisis/direct contact)?
- [ ] Named people and places (Pi, Lynton, the Jacksons, Alan, Paps of Jura, Loch Indaal)?

**Authentic:**
- [ ] Owner personality visible (specific details, preferences)?
- [ ] Honest about imperfections/tradeoffs?
- [ ] Natural speech patterns preserved (repetition, double modifiers, fragments)?

**Audience-aware (hub/guide pages):**
- [ ] Is the audience named? ("families especially", "for whisky groups")
- [ ] Are implications stated honestly? ("no car at the other end", "£500+ for a family")
- [ ] Does each audience know what to do next?

**Evocative (but specific):**
- [ ] Named geographic entities used (not just "views" — which views, of what)?
- [ ] One evocative phrase per paragraph max?
- [ ] Constraints framed as gifts where appropriate?

**Factually Consistent:**
- [ ] Walk times, ferry times, ratings match Key Facts table?
- [ ] Distillery count: 10 visitor-facing, 11 total (Laggan Bay caveat when needed)?
- [ ] Property names correct? Alan spelled correctly?

## Before/After Examples

### **Example 1: Hub Section**

❌ **Before (operational, audience-blind):**
> CalMac operates year-round sailings from Kennacraig. The crossing to Port Askaig takes 2 hours; to Port Ellen, 2 hours 20 minutes. Book 12 weeks ahead. Cancellations happen in rough weather, but we've helped over 600 guests navigate this journey. There's always a solution.

✅ **After (emotional, audience-aware, personal):**
> For most guests - families especially - the CalMac ferry from Kennacraig is how you'll get to Islay. And if you want to explore, Islay is a lot, lot easier with your own car.
>
> The ferry is actually one of the most enjoyable parts of the holiday. There's something genuinely romantic about getting on a boat with nothing else to do but watch the waves, the island getting ever bigger on the horizon, the Paps of Jura dominating the skyline. It feels like the holiday has properly started. We do this crossing all the time and it never loses its magic.
>
> Really important: ferries fill quickly in summer and do get cancelled in bad weather. Book early - as soon as you book your accommodation if you can - and sign up to CalMac alerts. And keep an eye on the weather, always keep an eye on the weather! If anything changes, contact us - we've never had a booking collapse, and we can often put you up in one of our other properties if you get stranded.

**What changed:** Removed route times and booking windows (spoke's job). Added audience framing. Named the Paps of Jura. Added "actually" to invert expectations. Kept the double "a lot, lot". Kept the repeated weather warning. Added specific offer (put you up in our other properties).

## Research & Verification Process

**Before asking Lynton for any fact or detail, do this first:**

### **1. Search existing content files**

The `cw/pbi/` directory is a large database of local knowledge, confirmed facts, and existing content. Search it before escalating to Lynton. Key files:

| What you need | Where to look |
|---------------|---------------|
| Distances, walk times, ferry times, ratings | `cw/pbi/CRITICAL-FACTS.md` |
| Existing block copy, place descriptions | `cw/pbi/content/CANONICAL-BLOCKS-MERGED-v4.md` |
| Property character, honest framing | `cw/pbi/nuance/PBI-NUANCE-BRIEF-ENHANCED.md` |
| Existing spoke page content | `cw/pbi/content/islay-travel/` and `cw/pbi/content/guides/` |
| Entity details (hours, contacts, descriptions) | `cw/pbi/content/` guide files and entity registers |

Also search the live site via Vercel browse or site search — content already published may contain details not yet in local files.

### **2. Web search for publicly available facts**

Before asking Lynton for general information, run a web search. This covers:

- Island statistics: coastline length, area, population (e.g. "Islay coastline miles")
- Website URLs: CalMac, Loganair, Citylink, islaygeology.org, Islay Coaches, bike hire operators
- Hotel and accommodation names at specific locations (e.g. "hotels Port Ellen Islay", "Port Askaig Hotel")
- Opening hours, seasonal schedules, prices for visitor attractions
- Any fact that a local guidebook or tourism site would carry

**Examples of questions that should never need Lynton:**
- "What is Islay's total coastline?" → web search
- "What's the URL for Islay geology?" → web search → islaygeology.org
- "What hotels are in Port Ellen for stranded ferry guests?" → web search → No. 1 Charlotte Street, Ardbeg House
- "Is the Port Askaig Hotel still trading?" → web search

### **3. Only escalate to Lynton for:**

- Personal opinions, preferences, and anecdotes ("which stop do you always make on the A83?")
- Insider local knowledge not available publicly ("what's the name Aileen's shop is also known as?")
- Local named details only a resident would know: the Screen Machine (roaming Highlands cinema truck), Mactaggart Leisure Centre (Bowmore pool), CalMac water bowls for dogs, Opera House Rocks, Sainagmore — these don't appear in guidebooks
- Editorial decisions (which distilleries to mention, what tone to take)
- Anything that requires Pi or Lynton's direct voice or experience

## Implementation Notes

### **For Content Creators:**

1. **Read this skill before every writing session**
2. **Search existing files and web before asking Lynton** — see Research & Verification Process above
3. **Open the gold standard files** — scopeIntro for hub/guide openers, Issue 8 rewrite for property pages
4. **Check the Key Facts table** for correct figures (walk times, ferry times, distillery counts)
5. **Ask:** "Would Pi actually say this to a guest in the kitchen?" If no, revise
6. **Default to "we"** — only use "I" for crisis comms or direct personal recommendations
7. **Do not smooth** natural speech patterns — repetition and double modifiers are features

### **For Quality Review:**

1. Read content aloud — does it sound human?
2. Is the audience named on hub pages?
3. Are natural speech patterns preserved?
4. Are named geographic entities used instead of generic descriptions?
5. Verify facts against Key Facts table

## Voice Summary Card

| Element | Our Way | Not This |
|---------|---------|----------|
| **Hub openers** | Lead with emotion | Lead with geography |
| **Description** | Named places ("Paps of Jura") | Generic ("beautiful views") |
| **Repetition** | Keep it ("always keep an eye on the weather!") | Smooth it out |
| **Modifiers** | Keep doubles ("a lot, lot easier") | Tidy to "a lot easier" |
| **Superlatives** | Understatement ("a pretty good ratio") | "Remarkable", "breathtaking" |
| **Audience** | Name them ("families especially") | Write for everyone |
| **Reassurance** | Specific offer ("put you up in our properties") | Generic ("we'll help") |
| **Default pronoun** | We, our | I (save for crisis/direct recs) |
| **Honesty** | "A bit tired, a lot loved" | [Hide imperfections] |
| **Sentences** | Mix: short/fragments welcome | Long, complex |
